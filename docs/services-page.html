Dental caries is the most common problem in dentistry. It represents a prolonged process of tooth tissue destruction. The cause of its occurrence is the negative impact of acid that forms in the oral cavity. As a result, hard tooth tissues are destroyed, and the inflammatory process spreads further into the soft area, forming a carious lesion.

How is Caries Treated?

It depends on its type and severity, and is carried out individually for each patient. This disease varies in stages and also in the areas of its occurrence. If it is advanced, it can lead to complications such as pulpitis, which can cause sharp shooting pains for the patient.

Treatment of Caries in Adults at Different Stages:

  • Surface. The initial stage of this disease is characterized by a small carious cavity and small areas of demineralization. In such cases, therapeutic agents based on phosphorus, calcium, and fluoride are used. They are applied to the tooth surface for additional protection. The infected cavity is then filled;
  • Medium. In this case, localized damage to tooth tissues occurs, and periodic pain may also occur. In such situations, the affected tissues are cleaned, and subsequent filling is performed. Local anesthesia may be used if necessary;
  • Deep. At this stage, extensive damage to tooth tissue occurs, sometimes affecting the nerve (sharp pain, purulent areas, gum swelling). Treatment of caries in such cases is more thorough and lengthy. It involves therapeutic procedures in tooth canals, cleaning the affected cavity, filling, and subsequent restoration of the tooth's shape.

Treatment of Caries in Children

When it comes to treating caries in children, this process requires timely diagnosis because the disease develops quite rapidly in such cases.

It is necessary to monitor the appearance of white or brown spots on the teeth of toddlers and older children, a painful reaction to very cold or hot food, and the occurrence of an unpleasant odor from the oral cavity.

If the child is diagnosed with the disease in the initial stage, serious intervention is not required. Treatment of caries in a child will be limited to protecting the enamel from further destruction using an effective and completely painless procedure: coating the tooth with fluoride silver or lacquer. When a child's permanent teeth appear, the dentist can seal the fissures between the protruding tooth parts to prevent the accumulation of bacteria that destroy the enamel.

In the case of disease progression, caries treatment in children involves filling. If not done, the disease will progress to a more acute form—pulpitis, and then to periodontitis.
